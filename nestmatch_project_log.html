Key Decisions

D01
Why not scrape realestate.com.au for listings?
Against their ToS and creates a brittle pipeline. Decision: manual curation first, PropTrack API later.
D02
Why no ML or LLM for ranking?
Explainability is the core product value. Decision: deterministic weighted scoring only in v1.
D03
Why C:\dev\ instead of OneDrive?
OneDrive interferes with Python venvs and Node.js. Decision: all code in C:\dev\, one source of truth.
D04
NestMatch vs DemandPilot as product name?
NestMatch is the buyer-facing product name. Decision: nestmatch.com.au registered. DemandPilot.com.au held for future platform layer.
D05
No auth, no maps, no AI chat in v1?
These add complexity before validating core value. Decision: input → ranked output → clear explanation. Nothing else.
D06
Why dropdowns instead of free-text inputs on the search form?
Free text requires parsing and validation complexity. Decision: constrained dropdowns in v1 — faster to build, impossible to break.
D07
Why remove is_new_build and replace with renovation_status?
A boolean is too blunt — a 1940s terrace fully renovated is more valuable than a 1990s apartment untouched. Decision: renovation_status with 4 values captures condition, not just age.
D08
NestMatch shows facts, not judgments — what does this mean in practice?
Scores power the ranking engine invisibly. What buyers see is always a fact: distances, commute times, sizes, zones. Never a score or a label like "great suburb". Decision: scores are internal only. All buyer-facing output is factual and verifiable.
D09
Why keep noise_score and suburb_lifestyle_score despite being subjective?
They differentiate properties that look identical on paper. Made optional — blank = engine ignores them. Decision: keep in schema, optional in data entry, invisible to buyers.
D10
Future idea: free-text buyer intake powered by Claude API?
"My daughter is becoming a teenager, needs train access independently" could auto-fill search criteria. Powerful differentiator. Decision: parked for Session 5 — validate core ranking first.
D11
Should NestMatch serve both residential and investment buyers, or focus on one?
The two types have fundamentally different decision criteria. Mixing them into one form creates a compromise product for both. Decision: two-mode entry point at launch — Residential and Investment — each with distinct search criteria and scoring logic.
D12
Residential buyers only care about lifestyle — do they need investment signals?
A residential buyer asks two questions simultaneously: "Will I love living here?" and "Will I be better off financially when I eventually sell?" NestMatch must answer both. Decision: residential mode includes suburb_trajectory (rising / stable / cooling), development_zone, and land_size_sqm as first-class signals — not afterthoughts.
D13
What happens to the Investment mode and DemandPilot.com.au?
Investment criteria (rental yield, vacancy rates, depreciation, employment hub proximity) are distinct enough to warrant a separate product. DemandPilot.com.au was initially considered as the investment-facing product name. ⚠ Overridden by D16 — both modes live at nestmatch.com.au.
D14
What is the single most important number for an investment buyer?
Capital gain potential first, rental yield second. Sydney is a growth market — investors prioritise long-term appreciation over income. Decision: investment scoring engine weights capital gain signals above yield signals.
D15
What are the key signals for capital gain potential in Sydney?
10-factor framework established: (1) Location economics — within 10–15km of CBD, proximity to multiple job nodes, train/metro spine; (2) Land scarcity — freestanding houses, low-density zoning R2, heritage areas; (3) Supply/demand imbalance — low days on market, high clearance rates; (4) Demographic upgrade — rising incomes, gentrification signals; (5) Infrastructure catalysts — new metro lines, hospitals, universities (but only if not yet priced in); (6) School catchments — top public school zones outperform; (7) Street-level signals — quiet streets, north-facing, rectangular blocks; (8) Price point liquidity — $1.5M–$2.5M houses have deepest buyer pool; (9) Rental demand — vacancy rates, yield stability as downside protection; (10) Story vs reality filter — owner-occupier demand and long-term holding patterns beat developer narratives. Decision: these 10 factors define the Investment mode scoring engine. New DB fields required: property_type_strata, vacancy_rate, days_on_market, auction_clearance_rate, heritage_overlay, aspect, flood_zone.
D16
Should Investment mode live at DemandPilot.com.au or nestmatch.com.au?
A separate domain splits brand recognition before either mode has been validated. One domain, two entry buttons is simpler to build and simpler for users to understand. DemandPilot.com.au remains held but unused for now. Decision: both Residential and Investment modes live at nestmatch.com.au — single brand, two-button entry point. Overrides D13.
D19
Should suburb_trajectory be a column on the properties table or a separate lookup table?
A column on properties requires updating every property row when market conditions change, and conflates suburb-level data with property-level data. A lookup table (suburb + property_type) is updated once per suburb/type combination and joined at query time — transparent to the engine. Townhouses inherit the house trajectory: transaction volumes are too thin in most suburbs for a reliable independent series. Minimum threshold: 8 transactions in the past 12 months — below this, no row is created and the engine uses neutral 0.5. Decision: standalone suburb_trajectories table with composite primary key (suburb, property_type). Townhouses map to house trajectory via CASE in the JOIN. Overrides the single-column approach attempted earlier in Session 5.
D20
What data should drive suburb_trajectory — subjective judgment or real data?
Manual assignment of rising/stable/cooling by feel violates D08 (facts, not judgments). Trajectory must be derived from transaction data with a fixed, reproducible methodology. Within a suburb, houses and apartments can move in opposite directions — a single suburb-level field is too blunt. Decision: trajectory is determined per suburb + property_type from median price change year-on-year (PropTrack or Domain public data). rising = >5% YoY, stable = -2% to +5%, cooling = <-2%. Minimum 8 transactions required. Fields stored: trajectory, median_price_change, transaction_volume, data_source, reference_period, updated_at.
D17
Should there be a minimum match threshold below which properties are not shown?
Showing low-percentage matches (e.g. 23%) is noise, not signal — it undermines the product's core promise. A fixed cut-off keeps results feeling curated. User-controlled threshold adds negligible backend complexity (one filter line post-scoring) but adds entry form friction if surfaced upfront. Decision: default threshold set at 60%. Properties scoring below 60% are not shown. Threshold is not exposed on the entry form — surfaced only on the results page as a "Show broader matches" slider.
D18
What is the homepage headline and subline?
The headline must convey NestMatch's differential — efficiency through matching, not a replacement for searching. The subline must be direct and set an expectation of speed. "Every property" was rejected as overpromising given manually curated inventory. Decision: headline locked as "Search smarter. Match faster." Subline locked as "Enter your criteria. Get your ranked shortlist in seconds."
D21
What is NestMatch — a search engine or a decision assistant?
Search engines return things that match filters. Decision assistants take a position on what to consider and why. NestMatch is a decision assistant: active, not passive — it does the reasoning, not the user. The output may look like a list of properties, but the reasoning layer underneath is categorically different. NestMatch must be able to defend every recommendation it makes. This distinction must be preserved in every product, design, and communication decision. Decision: NestMatch is positioned as a buyer-side intelligence layer — not a portal, not a filter engine. Every UI element, copy line, and data decision is tested against one question: "Does this help the user decide, or does it just help them browse?"
D22
What is the core differentiator that makes "decision assistant" real rather than a positioning claim?
Filter-based search assumes users already know what they want in structured form. Most buyers think in situations and constraints — conditional, emotional, implicit. Narrative input (free-text natural language intent) is the mechanism that translates this into ranked, explainable output. This is essentially what a good buyers agent charges $15k–$30k+ to do. NestMatch does it at scale. Filter UI is copyable in a sprint. A well-designed narrative input layer — with the interpretation logic behind it — is much harder to replicate. Decision: narrative input is the primary differentiator, not the scoring engine. Technical design of the narrative input layer is a Session 6 priority. The parked status from D10 is upgraded — narrative input is now a first-class product feature.
D23
What is NestMatch's genuine, durable competitive moat?
Data aggregation is not durable — anyone with capital can license CoreLogic or PropTrack. Scoring logic is copyable once visible. User preference modelling is real but only after significant volume. The only genuinely durable moat is decision history vs outcomes: tracking what users chose, versus what happened to those properties. This feedback loop compounds over years and cannot be bought or replicated quickly. Decision: data capture must be designed from day one to support this feedback loop — even before user volume makes it useful. Every search, every selection, and every outcome is a future training signal. This shapes how the DB is structured, what events are logged, and what gets tracked at every touchpoint.
D24
How should NestMatch handle uncertainty in its data and scores — especially suburb_trajectory?
Presenting scores with high confidence on uncertain data collapses trust instantly. Being wrong with apparent certainty is worse than being honest about uncertainty. This applies directly to suburb_trajectory: showing "rising" as a confident label without the underlying facts is false precision. The same risk applies to rental yields, vacancy rates, and any score derived from imperfect data. Decision: trajectory labels must always be accompanied by their source facts — e.g. "+13.9% YoY · 143 sales · CoreLogic Q1 2026". Labels are navigation aids; facts are the product. The UI must signal confidence ranges, not just rankings. NestMatch recommends — it never guarantees. Language discipline applies from day one.
