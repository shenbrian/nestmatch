Key Decisions

D01
Why not scrape realestate.com.au for listings?
Against their ToS and creates a brittle pipeline. Decision: manual curation first, PropTrack API later.
D02
Why no ML or LLM for ranking?
Explainability is the core product value. Decision: deterministic weighted scoring only in v1.
D03
Why C:\dev\ instead of OneDrive?
OneDrive interferes with Python venvs and Node.js. Decision: all code in C:\dev\, one source of truth.
D04
NestMatch vs DemandPilot as product name?
NestMatch is the buyer-facing product name. Decision: nestmatch.com.au registered. DemandPilot.com.au held for future platform layer.
D05
No auth, no maps, no AI chat in v1?
These add complexity before validating core value. Decision: input → ranked output → clear explanation. Nothing else.
D06
Why dropdowns instead of free-text inputs on the search form?
Free text requires parsing and validation complexity. Decision: constrained dropdowns in v1 — faster to build, impossible to break.
D07
Why remove is_new_build and replace with renovation_status?
A boolean is too blunt — a 1940s terrace fully renovated is more valuable than a 1990s apartment untouched. Decision: renovation_status with 4 values captures condition, not just age.
D08
NestMatch shows facts, not judgments — what does this mean in practice?
Scores power the ranking engine invisibly. What buyers see is always a fact: distances, commute times, sizes, zones. Never a score or a label like "great suburb". Decision: scores are internal only. All buyer-facing output is factual and verifiable.
D09
Why keep noise_score and suburb_lifestyle_score despite being subjective?
They differentiate properties that look identical on paper. Made optional — blank = engine ignores them. Decision: keep in schema, optional in data entry, invisible to buyers.
D10
Future idea: free-text buyer intake powered by Claude API?
"My daughter is becoming a teenager, needs train access independently" could auto-fill search criteria. Powerful differentiator. Decision: parked for Session 5 — validate core ranking first. Upgraded to first-class feature in D22.
D11
Should NestMatch serve both residential and investment buyers, or focus on one?
The two types have fundamentally different decision criteria. Decision: two-mode entry point at launch — Residential and Investment — each with distinct search criteria and scoring logic.
D12
Residential buyers only care about lifestyle — do they need investment signals?
A residential buyer asks two questions simultaneously: "Will I love living here?" and "Will I be better off financially when I eventually sell?" NestMatch must answer both. Decision: residential mode includes suburb_trajectory, development_zone, and land_size_sqm as first-class signals.
D13
What happens to the Investment mode and DemandPilot.com.au?
Investment criteria are distinct enough to warrant separate treatment. DemandPilot.com.au was initially considered as the investment-facing product name. ⚠ Overridden by D16 — both modes live at nestmatch.com.au.
D14
What is the single most important number for an investment buyer?
Capital gain potential first, rental yield second. Sydney is a growth market — investors prioritise long-term appreciation over income. Decision: investment scoring engine weights capital gain signals above yield signals.
D15
What are the key signals for capital gain potential in Sydney?
10-factor framework: (1) Location economics — within 10–15km of CBD; (2) Land scarcity — freestanding houses, R2 zoning; (3) Supply/demand imbalance — low days on market; (4) Demographic upgrade — gentrification signals; (5) Infrastructure catalysts — new metro lines; (6) School catchments; (7) Street-level signals — quiet, north-facing; (8) Price point liquidity — $1.5M–$2.5M; (9) Rental demand — vacancy rates; (10) Story vs reality filter — owner-occupier demand. Decision: these 10 factors define the Investment mode scoring engine.
D16
Should Investment mode live at DemandPilot.com.au or nestmatch.com.au?
A separate domain splits brand recognition before either mode has been validated. Decision: both Residential and Investment modes live at nestmatch.com.au — single brand, two-button entry point. Overrides D13.
D17
Should there be a minimum match threshold below which properties are not shown?
Showing low-percentage matches is noise, not signal. Decision: default threshold 60%. Not exposed on entry form — surfaced only on results page as "Show broader matches" slider.
D18
What is the homepage headline and subline?
Decision: headline locked as "Search smarter. Match faster." Subline: "Enter your criteria. Get your ranked shortlist in seconds."
D19
Should suburb_trajectory be a column on the properties table or a separate lookup table?
A column conflates suburb-level data with property-level data and requires updating every property row when market conditions change. Decision: standalone suburb_trajectories table with composite primary key (suburb, property_type). Townhouses map to house trajectory via CASE in the JOIN.
D20
What data should drive suburb_trajectory — subjective judgment or real data?
Manual assignment violates D08. Decision: trajectory determined per suburb + property_type from median price change YoY. rising = >5%, stable = -2% to +5%, cooling = <-2%. Stored as decimal (e.g. 0.139 = +13.9%).
D21
What is NestMatch — a search engine or a decision assistant?
Search engines return things that match filters. Decision assistants take a position on what to consider and why. Decision: NestMatch is a buyer-side intelligence layer. Every UI element is tested against: "Does this help the user decide, or just help them browse?"
D22
What is the core differentiator that makes "decision assistant" real rather than a positioning claim?
Filter UI is copyable in a sprint. A well-designed narrative input layer with interpretation logic is much harder to replicate. Decision: narrative input is the primary differentiator. It does at scale what a good buyers agent charges $15k–$30k+ to do.
D23
What is NestMatch's genuine, durable competitive moat?
Data aggregation is not durable. Scoring logic is copyable. The only genuinely durable moat is decision history vs outcomes: tracking what users chose versus what happened to those properties. Decision: data capture must be designed from day one to support this feedback loop. Every search, selection, and outcome is a future training signal.
D24
How should NestMatch handle uncertainty in its data and scores?
Being wrong with apparent certainty is worse than being honest about uncertainty. Decision: trajectory labels must always carry source facts — e.g. "+13.9% YoY · CoreLogic Q1 2026". NestMatch recommends — it never guarantees.
D25
What copy should the two mode cards carry on the homepage?
Decision: Residential — "Live well and build equity. Matched to your budget, commute, schools, and suburb trajectory." Investment — "Optimize returns. Matched to your goals for capital gain and rental income." Locked after four visual iterations (Options A→D).
D26
Should land_size_sqm_min be added as a residential search filter?
Tester 5 had a single hard constraint — 700sqm minimum land — with no suburb preference. For duplex rebuild, development-zone, and large-family buyers, land size is often the primary filter. Decision: add land_size_sqm_min as optional filter. Hard filter in engine.py before soft scoring — a property below minimum is excluded entirely, not penalised.
D27
How should the narrative parser handle sparse or keyword-style input?
Sparse input like "4br house good schools inner west 1.5M" is legitimate buyer behaviour — it mirrors how people search on Google and REA. Decision: update parse-narrative system prompt — any clearly implied field must extract at high confidence regardless of sentence structure. Low confidence is reserved for genuinely ambiguous intent, not sparse formatting.
D28
How should tradeoffs be displayed on the results page?
Tester 2 found multiple amber flags without hierarchy reads as noise, not signal. Decision: maximum 2 tradeoffs per property card, ordered by severity. Each tradeoff is one plain-English sentence with a concrete fact — e.g. "35-min commute exceeds your 30-min preference". Additional tradeoffs collapsed behind "Show more".
D29
Should NestMatch surface actionable property details to close the loop for buyers?
NestMatch currently ranks and explains properties but provides no path to action — buyers cannot find the address, contact the agent, book an inspection, or access the contract. A ranked shortlist with no exit is a dead end. The minimum viable actionable set is: street address, agent name, agent phone, and listing URL. With these four fields a buyer can drive past, call the agent, book an inspection, and request a contract. Linking to REA and Domain both where applicable — no exclusivity at this stage. A future "I bought this" feedback mechanism should be scoped in Session 8 to begin capturing outcome data per D23. Decision: add address, agent_name, agent_phone, and listing_url to the properties schema. Populate for all existing properties. Surface on the result card as "View listing →" and "Contact agent →". Session 8 priority — 40 actionable properties beats 50 dead-end ones.
